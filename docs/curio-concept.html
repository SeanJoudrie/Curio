01

The idea, in one loop

Instead of infinite content, Curio gives you one thing to actually go do. You tell it how much time, money, and energy you've got right now; it hands you a single physical-world challenge pulled from a specimen drawer of hundreds; you do it, badly or brilliantly; you rate how it felt; and once a day, you can turn the result into a shareable card. The whole point is that trying is the win — skill is a side effect.

02

What v0.1 was missing

Nine gaps, found by pressure-testing the loop against real use — a rainy day, a person who hates cooking, a skill that can't be finished in one sitting. Each one gets a concrete fix, not just a flag.

Gap — one-size deck

No way to lean toward or away from a category

Fixed by §04 Boost & mute — an explicit per-category dial, plus quiet learning from your ratings, so a cooking-lover sees more Culinary and someone who hates it can starve that drawer without deleting it.

Gap — false one-shot

Sourdough doesn't fit in a day

Added a multi-day challenge type — a short list of dated stages instead of one task. See the sourdough card in §05.

Gap — dead end

Nothing after "you finished the task"

Every challenge now carries a Resources & inspiration field — a real example of what a good (and a funny) result looks like, plus one concrete next step for anyone who wants to go deeper than one card.

Gap — deck-or-nothing

No way to browse without swiping

A separate Catalog tab: the full flat list, searchable and filterable by drawer and budget — the sortable "list of 100" you originally asked for, living alongside the swipe deck rather than instead of it.

Gap — brittle plans

What if it rains on the bird photo, or you're out of eggs?

A one-tap Reroll swaps in a same-budget challenge without breaking your streak — capped at 2 rerolls a day so it doesn't become a way to dodge every hard card.

Gap — silent app

Nothing brings you back tomorrow

A single gentle, timeable daily nudge ("today's curio is ready") — one notification, no streak-shaming, snoozable per day. This is the whole replace-the-scroll mechanic; without it the loop just stops.

Gap — one body assumed

Cartwheels and photography assume full mobility and vision

Skills with a physical or sensory assumption carry an accessible-alternate task alongside the default one — same micro-lesson, a task that doesn't require the assumed ability.

Gap — no guardrail

Fire, knives, and "forage something edible" need a warning, not a wall of legal text

A one-line safety note field, shown inline on the card itself for anything involving heat, blades, or wildlife — short enough to actually read.

Gap — lonely log

Your results only ever get seen by you or, once a day, your own feed

An opt-in Inspiration Gallery — anonymized results from other people who did the exact same challenge, browsable before or after you try it, separate from the once-a-day personal share.

03

Finding today's challenge

Three filters, always visible, never buried in a menu: time (10 min / an hour / all afternoon), budget ($0 / under $20 / splurge), and plans (staying in / running errands / already out). Set them once and the deck reshuffles to match — "no plans, no budget, twenty minutes" pulls up bird photography and card tricks, not woodworking and sourdough. Your Boost & mute settings (§04) reorder the deck underneath these filters automatically — they don't need their own chip here, that's what keeps this screen from turning into a settings panel.

04

Personalizing the deck: boost & mute

This was the biggest hole in v0.1: no way to say "give me more of this" or "I never want to see that." Tune your deck is a settings screen listing every drawer with a three-state dial — boosted (shows up more often), default, or muted (removed from the daily deck entirely, but still sitting in the Catalog if you ever want it back — nothing is deleted, just deprioritized). Explicit taps set it directly; every 1–5 stamp you give also nudges the dial a little on its own, so the deck gets more personal even if you never open this screen.

05

Anatomy of a challenge card

Every entry pairs a two-minute micro-lesson (the actual skill — rule of thirds, a grammar rule, a knife grip) with one concrete task you can finish today, and now a resources & inspiration field so there's a next step once the card is done. Encouragement copy is written in up front, not bolted on after — see §09 for the rules behind it.

Photography & Visual Arts

Shoot a bird using the rule of thirds

PHO-014
Micro-lesson
Split the frame into a 3×3 grid. Put your subject on one of the four crossing points instead of dead center — the eye reads it as more alive, less like a mugshot.
Task
Find any bird — pigeon counts — and take 5 shots with it off-center. Keep your favorite.
Accessible alternate
Can't get outdoors or track a moving subject? Reframe any still object in the room the same way — a mug, a plant, a shoe by the door.
Encouragement
Blurry, too far away, flew off mid-shot — that's the whole exercise. You're training your eye, not auditioning for National Geographic.
Resources & inspiration
Good and funny both count: a perfectly-framed pigeon on a wire, or a squirrel that photobombed the whole shot. Go deeper by trying the same grid on a person or a plate of food tomorrow.

Three Portuguese phrases for the street

LAN-002
Micro-lesson
Portuguese softens its "s" at the end of words into a "sh" sound — "dois" sounds like "doysh." Say it out loud before you read on.
Task
Learn "obrigado/a" (thanks), "quanto custa?" (how much?), and "com licença" (excuse me). Use all three on someone today, even if it's the barista.
Encouragement
Mangling the accent is part of learning it. Nobody has ever been mad at someone for trying.
Resources & inspiration
Say all three phrases into a voice memo before and after using them for real — most people can already hear themselves improve. Go deeper: this same skill has a 30-day sprint version if three phrases wasn't enough.

Home Fermentation & Brewing

Sourdough starter — flour to first loaf

FER-001
Micro-lesson
Flour and water left out attract wild yeast and bacteria already floating in your kitchen air — you're not adding life to it, you're inviting it in. Feeding it daily just keeps that colony fed faster than it starves.
Task — multi-day
  • DAY 1Mix equal parts flour and water in a jar. Loosely cover. Leave it out.
  • DAY 2Discard half, feed with fresh flour and water. Nothing's happening yet — that's normal.
  • DAY 3–4Watch for bubbles and a sour smell. Keep feeding once a day.
  • DAY 5It should double in size a few hours after feeding. Use the discard for pancakes as a first win before committing to a full loaf.
Encouragement
A flat, grey, weird-smelling starter on day 2 is not a failure report — it's day 2. Most starters look unpromising right up until they don't.
Resources & inspiration
Real starters range from soupy and beige to thick and streaky — there's no "correct" look, only "is it bubbling." Go deeper: search "sourdough starter troubleshooting" the first time it does something confusing, which it will.

Bake a cake from scratch

CUL-009
Micro-lesson
Creaming butter and sugar together traps air — that's what makes a cake rise without help. Beat it until it's pale and fluffy, not just mixed.

06

After the challenge: the stamp, not the score

No pass/fail, no correctness grading — just one honest question: how did that feel? A 1–5 stamp goes into a personal log (private by default) that becomes a growing record of things you've actually tried, with photo attached if you took one. Streaks count attempts, not perfect scores, so a "2" still keeps your streak alive — quitting halfway is the only thing that breaks it.

07

Sharing: once a day, low stakes, watermarked

You can turn one completed challenge per day into a share card — a deliberate cap, so it never turns into a feed you're performing for. The card carries a small, permanent Curio watermark, built to travel well when it's re-posted to Stories, group chats, or anywhere else. The pitch to the viewer is always "here's a weird thing I tried today," never "look how good I am at this" — funny results get the exact same card treatment as great ones.

08

The catalog

Under the hood, a Skill is a category of ability ("bird photography basics"); a Challenge is one concrete, budget-tagged task that teaches a sliver of it. Most skills start with one challenge; popular ones grow a short ladder (challenge 2 of "bird photography" might be shooting into backlight). Two additions since v0.1: a resources field on every challenge, and a preference record per user per drawer, so boosting or muting a category (§04) has somewhere to live.

// one entry in the catalog
Skill {
  id: "PHO"                    // drawer code
  name: "Photography & Visual Arts"
  oneLiner: "See the ordinary world through a frame"
}

Challenge {
  id: "PHO-014"
  skillId: "PHO"
  title: "Shoot a bird using the rule of thirds"
  microLesson: string          // the 2-minute lesson
  task: string                 // the concrete thing to do today
  stages?: { day: string, task: string }[]   // present only on multi-day challenges
  accessibleAlternate?: string // same lesson, a task without the assumed ability
  safetyNote?: string          // one line, only on heat/blade/wildlife challenges
  encouragement: string        // pre-written, low-pressure framing
  resources: {
    inspiration: string        // what a good AND a funny result look like
    goDeeper: string           // one concrete next step, not a link dump
  }
  budget: {
    time: "15m" | "1h" | "half-day" | "multi-day"
    cost: "free" | "$" | "$$"
    setting: "home" | "outdoors" | "errand"
  }
  funnyResultsExpected: true   // flags copy that leans into imperfect outcomes
  shareTemplate: string        // pre-filled caption for the share card
}

UserPreference {
  userId: string
  skillId: string              // drawer code, e.g. "CUL"
  affinity: -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 // -2 = muted, +2 = boosted, set explicitly or nudged by ratings
}

09

Voice: encouraging, never precious

The single job of every line of copy is to make someone who has never done the thing feel safe trying it badly. That means naming the bad outcome before they can worry about it, and treating "funny" as a legitimate win condition, not a consolation prize.

Avoid

"Master the rule of thirds and take a stunning bird photograph."

Instead

"Blurry, too far, flew off mid-shot — that's the whole exercise. You're training your eye, not auditioning for National Geographic."

Avoid

"Follow the recipe exactly for best results."

Instead

"Sunken middle, burnt edge, tastes incredible anyway — funny results are a completely valid outcome. Rate it a 5 if you laughed."

10

Open questions, before this becomes code

Things worth deciding on purpose rather than by default, once this concept feels right:

Scope

Where does it live?

New app entirely, or a section bolted onto an existing project? Decides the tech stack and design system from day one.

Depth

How many challenges are hand-written before launch?
