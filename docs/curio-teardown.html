PRODUCT TEARDOWN · CURIO v0.2 · UNSPONSORED, UNSPARING

The Curio Teardown

One reviewer, no mercy, every metric scored 1–10. What it nails, what it fumbles, and exactly how to fix the fumbles — with the reasoning, not just the note.
5.9Composite

A 9/10 idea currently executed at a ~6. The concept is genuinely lovely and the voice is a real asset. But four holes drag the average down hard: no monetization, no onboarding, no plan for authoring content at scale, and a shaky answer to the one question that matters most — can it actually intercept the moment you reach for the phone to scroll? Fix those four and this is an 8+.

01

Concept & strategy

Is the fundamental bet sound, and does it have a reason to exist and survive?

Core value proposition8/10

"Trade one scroll for one thing you actually did." That's a clean, emotionally true pitch and it's rare — most apps sell more, this one sells less, better. The "trying is the win, skill is a side effect" framing is the whole soul of the product and it's right.

Last mile Put that sentence on the box. The current tagline ("a daily cabinet of curiosities") is pretty but abstract; lead with the transformation — "You'll have done something today" beats "a cabinet of curiosities" for a stranger deciding whether to install.
Why App-store conversion is won on the promise of a changed you, not a described product. Duolingo doesn't sell "a language SRS engine," it sells "you, bilingual." Same move.
Doomscroll-replacement fit5/10

This is the load-bearing wall and right now it's cracked. Scrolling wins because it's zero-effort, zero-setup, and available in the exact low-energy moments you reach for it — in bed, on the toilet, in a waiting room. Curio's challenges ("bake a cake," "photograph a bird") mostly cannot be done in those moments. You're not competing with Instagram; you're competing with the couch. Right now Curio asks more energy than the moment it's trying to hijack has to give.

The fix Add a third challenge tier: "Right Now" micro-curios — 60-to-120-second things doable one-handed, in bed, phone-only: learn one word, one breath drill, one mental-math trick, "notice five sounds," one card-shuffle move, identify one constellation out the window. When the app opens it leads with a Right-Now card first, and the bigger budgeted challenges are the "got more? go bigger" tier.
Why Behavior substitution only works when the replacement fits the same context as the habit. You can't beat a phone-in-bed habit with a go-outside solution. A Right-Now tier meets the scroll where it actually lives, earns the streak in that moment, and then upsells effort — the reverse of the current order.
Differentiation / moat6/10

The neighborhood is crowded — Finch, Habitica, Pinterest, BeReal, Skillshare, "1 Second Everyday," every habit tracker. The closest cousin is BeReal (once-a-day, low-stakes, anti-performative) crossed with a challenge engine. The differentiators are real but copyable: curated challenges, budget-sort, and the anti-perfectionist voice. Content can be cloned in a weekend by a competitor with a content team.

The fix Build the moat out of the two things that can't be copied cheaply: (1) a proprietary, personality-drenched content library so deep and so consistently funny/kind that the voice itself is the brand (see Duolingo's owl, Cards Against Humanity); (2) network effects via the gallery — "1,400 people also botched this cake" is data a fresh competitor can't fake on day one.
Why In content products the moat is rarely the mechanic (swiping is free to clone) — it's accumulated catalog + accumulated community. Both compound over time and punish late entrants. Lean into whichever you can start compounding first; catalog is the safer bet.
Monetization model2/10

Completely absent from the spec, and this one's a founder-killer. Hand-authored, fact-checked, legally-safe content is expensive to produce and maintain, and there's currently zero revenue against it. "Free, no sign-up" is a lovely user promise and a terrible business model with no plan behind it.

The fix Freemium with the growth loop protected: free = today's challenge, Right-Now tier, the daily share, basic streak. Curio+ (subscription) = the full browsable catalog, boost/mute controls, multi-day tracks, extra rerolls, past-log photo storage, seasonal packs. Plus ethical affiliate — the cake needs ingredients, the whittling needs a knife; a disclosed "here's a starter kit" link is genuinely useful and revenue. Keep sharing 100% free forever — it's the acquisition engine, never gate it.
Why Gate depth and control, never the core daily loop or the share. The daily habit builds the attachment that makes people pay for depth; the free share recruits the next user. Affiliate aligns incentives — you were going to tell them what to buy anyway. (You already have a "Supporter" pattern in your other app — reuse the muscle.)
Naming & brand7/10

"Curio" is genuinely good — curiosity + cabinet-of-curiosities, and the collection metaphor is gold (more on that under Streaks). But it's crowded: "Curio" and "Curious" are already used by several apps/AI products, and the .app domain and clean trademark are unlikely to be free. SEO will be a knife-fight.

The fix Do a trademark + app-store search before you fall in love. Strong on-theme alternates that mean "to try a little of many things": Dabble (near-perfect — dabbling is the product), Tinker, Forage, Lark ("do it for a lark"), Rummage, Sundry, Whim, Gambit. "Dabble" is the one to beat.
Why A name you can actually own (trademark + domain + top app-store result) is worth more than a slightly-prettier name you'll fight for forever. "Dabble" also describes the behavior, which quietly does marketing work every time it's said.
02

Core loop & UX

Once you're in, does the machine feel good to operate — and does it get out of its own way?

The daily loop (S1–S5)8/10

Set budget → swipe → do → rate → share is tight, legible, and complete. It has a clear beginning and a satisfying end, which most habit apps lack. No notes on the skeleton.

Last mile The loop has no explicit "come back tomorrow" beat baked into S5. End the share/stamp step with a tiny forward hook — "tomorrow's is already picked" or a peek at the drawer it'll come from — so the loop closes by opening the next one.
Why Habit loops need a cue for the next iteration planted inside the reward of the current one (Nir Eyal's "Hooked"). A closed loop with no forward hook is a great one-time experience and a weak habit.
Onboarding / first run2/10

There isn't any. The doc drops a new user straight into a deck of 381 unfamiliar things with no priors, no first win, and no reason established. First-session completion is where 90% of these apps die, and right now it's a blank.

The fix A 30-second, three-tap taste quiz — "Which pull at you?" (pick a few), "Which would you never?" (pick a few) — which seeds initial boost/mute so the deck is warm on card one. Then force a guaranteed first win in the first session: a 90-second Right-Now curio you can finish before you ever close the app. Stamp it, and then introduce sharing/streaks.
Why "Time to first value" is the single biggest predictor of D1 retention. A completed challenge in session one converts "an app I downloaded" into "a thing I did" — the exact identity shift the product is selling — before the user has any chance to bounce.
Swipe deck interaction6/10

Fun and on-brand, but three issues: (1) three-direction swipe (left/right/up) has a learning curve and up-swipe fights OS gestures; (2) "skip forever-ish" can silently strip the catalog for a heavy skipper; (3) a swipe deck is itself a browsing surface — the risk that the anti-scroll app grows its own little scroll.

The fix Make tap-to-open the primary action and swipe an accelerator, not the only path. Skip = deprioritize, never delete (it can resurface in a month). And cap the deck: show a small, finite hand (say 5–7 cards) matched to today's budget, not an endless stack — a hand of cards is a decision, an infinite stack is a scroll.
Why A bounded choice set (Barry Schwartz, "paradox of choice") prevents the browse-instead-of-do failure mode and keeps the product honest to its own thesis. Finite decks feel like dealing cards; infinite decks feel like TikTok.
Choice vs. serendipity balance5/10

Your original dream was "what should I do today" — one answer, no agonizing. But v0.2's filters + swipe deck + boost/mute reintroduce a lot of choosing. There's a real tension between "surprise me with a random skill" (the magic) and "let me sort and filter" (the control), and the doc tries to have both without picking a default.

The fix Pick a hero: "Today's Curio" is one assigned card (respecting budget + preferences) shown big on open — the Wordle move, one thing, decided for you. The deck becomes the secondary "not feeling it? deal me another hand" escape hatch. Serendipity is the default; control is the fallback.
Why Decision fatigue is the enemy of a daily ritual. Wordle, BeReal, and the NYT daily games all win by removing the choice. Give the anxious-at-a-menu majority one confident answer, and keep the deck for the minority who want to shop.
Budget filters (time / money / plans)7/10

The standout original idea and still one of the best things here — "sort by what I can actually afford right now" is exactly how real humans decide what to do with an evening. Three axes is the right number.

The fix Add a fourth implicit axis the app fills in for free: context (time of day, weather, indoors/outdoors) — see the Context Awareness metric below. And let budgets be saved as one-tap moods: "Cozy night in," "Killing 15 min," "Out and about" — so setting the budget is one tap, not three.
Why Presets collapse a 3-dial setup into a single recognizable intent, which is how people actually think ("I want a cozy night," not "time=60, cost=$, plans=in"). Lower friction on the filter = more people reach the deck.
Personalization — boost & mute8/10

Exactly the right response to "let cooking-lovers get more cooking, let cooking-haters kill it." Three-state dial + quiet learning from ratings is the correct two-layer design (explicit control on top of implicit inference). Solid.

Last mile Guard the failure mode: a user who mutes everything they don't instantly like ends up with a thin, samey deck and churns. Add a "wildcard" dial — a user-set % of the deck that ignores preferences to keep discovery alive — defaulted on, because discovering-your-hobby is the whole point.
Why Pure preference-optimization creates filter bubbles that feel stale and quietly kill the "discover a hobby you didn't know you'd love" promise. A protected exploration budget is how Spotify/Discover Weekly keeps recommendations from collapsing inward.
Context awareness (weather / time / place)3/10

Almost entirely absent, and it's a missed layup. The app suggests "photograph a bird outside" while it's raining and dark, or "watch a sunrise" at 9pm. A challenge app that doesn't know it's raining feels dumb in a way users notice immediately.

The fix Tag every challenge with context requirements (daylight, dry, outdoors, quiet) and filter the deck against device time + a lightweight weather lookup. "It's pouring — here's an indoor one" is a five-word line that makes the app feel alive.
Why Contextual relevance is the cheapest possible way to feel intelligent and cared-for. It costs one weather API call and a few tags, and it's the difference between "this app gets my life" and "this app is a static list." Small lift, big perceived-intelligence payoff.
03

Content — the actual product

This app is its content. If the catalog is thin or generic, nothing else matters. This is where the real company lives.

Catalog breadth9/10

381 skills across 24 drawers, and the range is genuinely delightful — sourdough next to sleight-of-hand next to birdwatching next to needle-felting. This is the "fuck-tons of skills" vision realized, and the taxonomy is sensible. Best-in-class here.

Last mile Two thin spots: no drawer for the very-low-energy "Right Now" tier (see Doomscroll Fit) and light coverage of solo-emotional / relationship / parenting / pet skills. Add a "5-minute mind" drawer and a "people in your life" drawer and you've covered the last obvious gaps.
Why Breadth is the discovery engine — the more unexpected drawers, the more "huh, I'd never have tried that" moments, which is the emotional core. The only breadth worth adding now is breadth that serves the two weakest use-contexts (no energy, and connecting with people).
Content depth / authoring at scale4/10

Here's the iceberg under the waterline. 381 names is not 381 challenges. Each needs a micro-lesson, task, accessible alternate, safety note, encouragement, and resources — call it ~6 carefully-written, fact-checked, liability-aware strings each. That's 2,000+ pieces of hand-crafted copy, plus upkeep. This isn't an app project; it's a content operation, and the doc doesn't reckon with the labor.

The fix Three moves: (1) Launch narrow and deep — 40–60 fully, lovingly authored challenges spread one-or-two per drawer, not one drawer finished. (2) Build an authoring pipeline: a strict template + an AI-assisted first draft that a human always edits for voice, facts, and safety — never ship raw generation. (3) Open community submissions with moderation as the long-tail scaling path once you have a house style to hold them to.
Why Depth beats breadth for retention — 50 challenges people love outperform 381 that feel like a wiki. Narrow-and-deep also lets you find the voice before you scale it, so the AI-assisted pipeline has a target to imitate. Community content is the only way "hundreds and hundreds" stays fed without a full-time writers' room forever.
Challenge card design8/10

The micro-lesson + task + encouragement + resources structure is excellent — it teaches, then acts, then reassures, then points onward. The sourdough multi-day card proves the format flexes. Strong information design.

Last mile Add a "what you'll need" checklist before commit (ingredients, tools) so nobody starts a cake and discovers they're out of eggs at step 3. And a one-tap "I'm in" that starts a soft timer/tracker, so the card isn't just read, it's entered.
Why The gap between "sounds fun" and "actually started" is where challenges die. A pre-commit checklist removes the mid-task rage-quit, and an explicit start state creates the commitment device that makes finishing (and rating) more likely.
Time & cost estimate honesty5/10

The estimates are optimistic to the point of dishonest, and since budget is a core sort, wrong numbers break the whole promise. "60 min" for a from-scratch cake is fiction (20 prep + 35 bake + cool ≈ 2 hr). "$8–12" assumes an empty pantry. When the challenge blows the budget the user explicitly set, trust evaporates.

The fix Split time into "hands-on" vs. "total elapsed" (every recipe app learned this the hard way), and define cost as "extra spend assuming a basic pantry," with the what-you'll-need checklist showing what you probably already have. Round up, always — under-promise, over-deliver.
Why A budget filter is a contract: "you said 20 minutes." Breaking it once teaches the user to distrust every number in the app, which poisons the single most differentiated feature you have. Honest, slightly-generous estimates are cheap insurance on your best idea.
Voice & copy9/10

The best-executed thing in the entire doc. "Name the bad outcome before they can worry about it" is a real writing philosophy, and "rate it a 5 if you laughed" is the kind of line that becomes a brand. This voice is a genuine competitive asset — it's the hardest thing here to copy.

Last mile Write it down as an enforceable style guide (do/don't pairs, banned words like "master"/"perfect"/"stunning," the funny-is-a-win rule) before anyone else — or any AI draft — touches the catalog. The voice only stays an asset if it survives scaling.
Why Voice is the one moat competitors can't clone in a weekend, but it's also the first thing that dies when you scale authoring to many writers. A codified style guide is what let Duolingo, Mailchimp, and Slack keep a consistent voice across hundreds of contributors.
Multi-day challenge handling6/10

Good instinct to add it (sourdough genuinely can't be a one-shot), and the staged format is clean. But it collides with the daily loop and the doc doesn't resolve it: if I'm on day 3 of sourdough, is that my curio today? Do I also get a fresh card? Does feeding the starter keep my streak? Undefined.

The fix Multi-day challenges run as a pinned "in-progress" tracker on the home screen and do not consume your daily new-card slot. Today's tiny step (feed the starter) counts toward your streak, and you can still deal a fresh short challenge. One commitment shouldn't block all discovery.
Why Long projects and daily novelty serve different needs (mastery vs. exploration) and shouldn't compete for the same slot. Letting them coexist means a user can have a slow-burn sourdough and still get their daily hit of "something new" — which is what keeps both alive.
04

Engagement & social

What brings people back tomorrow, and what makes them bring a friend?

Rating system (1–5 stamp)8/10

"How did that feel" instead of "how well did you do" is exactly right — it removes performance pressure and doubles as the preference signal that powers boost/mute. One elegant input, two jobs. Love it.

Last mile Add an optional one-tap tag on the low scores — a "2" could mean "boring," "too hard," or "not my thing," and those route very differently (mute the drawer vs. offer an easier variant vs. just bad luck). One extra tap turns a blunt number into a useful signal.
Why A raw 1–5 is ambiguous for personalization — a low score from "too hard" should trigger an easier challenge, not mute the whole category. Disambiguating why makes the deck smarter without adding real friction.
Streak mechanic5/10

Internally contradictory. The doc promises "no streak-shaming" and then centers a 🔥12-day streak that "quitting halfway breaks." That's the exact anxiety-engine (Duolingo's infamous guilt) that this calm, anti-doomscroll app claims to be an antidote to. You can't sell serenity and run a streak that punishes a missed day.

The fix Demote consecutive-day streaks and promote the collection. You're literally building a cabinet of curiosities — make the hero metric "47 skills tried," a shelf that only ever grows and never breaks. Keep a soft streak with free "rest days" and auto-freezes, framed as a gentle rhythm, never a chain you're about to snap.
Why Loss-aversion streaks drive engagement and anxiety, and the anxiety is off-brand here to the point of hypocrisy. A growth-only collection (Pokédex, a filling shelf) gives the same "keep going" pull through accumulation instead of threat — and it fits the Curio metaphor so well it feels inevitable.
Sharing / virality engine7/10

Once-a-day cap + watermark + "here's a weird thing I tried, not look how good I am" is a smart, BeReal-flavored, anti-performative design, and the watermark makes every share a growth ad. Good engine.

The fix The watermark alone is a weak install driver — nobody types a URL off a Story. Make the shared card carry a "try this exact challenge" deep link / QR so a friend lands on the same challenge, pre-loaded. And add "challenge a friend" as a first-class action — the single highest-intent viral loop you have.
Why Virality needs a frictionless path from view to install to value, not just brand exposure. A deep link that drops the friend onto the exact cake you botched turns "cute post" into "oh, I'll try that too" in one tap — that's the difference between a watermark and an actual K-factor.
Inspiration Gallery4/10

Lovely idea ("1,400 people also botched this cake" is reassuring and on-message) but the doc waves away a brutal operational reality: user-submitted photos = content moderation, NSFW, spam, harassment, and — if minors are on the app — serious legal exposure (CSAM scanning, COPPA). This is a whole trust-and-safety function hiding behind one nice sentence.

The fix Do not ship open photo submission at launch. Start curated-only (staff-picked examples baked into each card) or text/stats-only ("73% rated this a 4 or 5; top reflection: …"). Graduate to user photos only once you have moderation tooling, reporting, an age gate, and automated CSAM/NSFW scanning in place.
Why UGC image platforms live or die on trust-and-safety, and the failure mode isn't "bad UX," it's "legal catastrophe / app-store ban." Curated-first gives you 80% of the reassurance value at ~0% of the risk, and buys time to build the safety stack before you need it.
Retention past the novelty cliff5/10

Discovery apps have a predictable arc: magical for two weeks, then the cards feel same-y, you've muted what you hate, the deck thins, and you drift. The doc's depth mechanics (multi-day, resources) help but don't fully answer "why open it in week 8?"

The fix Three engines: (1) seasonal/event/weather packs so the catalog feels alive and dated, not static; (2) the "you might have found your thing" insight — "you've rated 5/5 on six cooking curios; want a guided cooking path?" — converting discovery into depth, which is the emotional payoff the whole pitch promised; (3) light social (a friend's daily curio, a weekly group theme).

Why Novelty decays; identity and relationships don't. Turning "I tried cooking" into "I'm getting into cooking" (identity) or "my friends and I do this" (social) are the two durable retention drivers once the surprise wears off — and the hobby-discovery insight is the exact "discover your hobby" dream you started with, finally paid off.
Re-engagement (notifications + widget)6/10

A single gentle daily nudge is the right tone, but making it the only re-engagement path is fragile — iOS notification opt-in hovers around 40–60%, so up to half your users never get the one signal you're relying on to bring them back.

The fix A home-screen widget showing today's curio is the killer move — it's passive, needs no notification permission, and literally sits on the home screen next to the apps you're trying to replace. Pair with optional email and a "morning ritual" pairing (with your coffee), so re-engagement doesn't hinge on one permission dialog.
Why Widgets intercept the exact moment of the scroll habit — the home-screen glance — without asking permission, and they turn the app from "a thing I open" into "a thing that's there." Diversifying re-engagement past notifications is how you survive the users who tap "Don't Allow."
Verification / integrity6/10

There's no way for the app to know you actually made the cake — it's all self-report. For a personal-growth app that's fine (you only cheat yourself), but it quietly caps any feature that needs trust: leaderboards, competitive streaks, rewards.

The fix Don't build verification — make honesty the design stance. Frame the optional photo as "evidence for future-you," not proof for the system. And never ship features (competitive leaderboards, prizes) that would give people a reason to lie. Keep the stakes internal and the integrity question never arises.
Why Verification is expensive, invasive, and antithetical to a calm, trust-based app. The right move isn't to police cheating — it's to design a game where cheating has no payoff, so the whole problem dissolves instead of needing a solution.
05

Systems: accessibility, safety, privacy, build

The unglamorous stuff that sinks apps quietly if it's ignored.

Accessibility7/10

The "accessible alternate task" idea is genuinely thoughtful and ahead of most apps — it treats accessibility as content design, not just a checkbox. Good instinct.

The fix Broaden past the alternate-task: swipe needs a tap-button equivalent (motor accessibility), screen-reader labels on every card, respected system font-scaling, and reduced-motion support. And write alternates for sensory assumptions too (audio challenges for the Deaf, visual for the blind), not just mobility.
Why Gesture-only UIs exclude motor-impaired and screen-reader users entirely, and it's also just an app-store review risk. Accessibility done at the content layer and the interaction layer is both the right thing and a moat — almost no competitor bothers, so it's a differentiator too.
Safety & liability5/10

The one-line inline safety note is the right instinct (nobody reads a wall of legal text), but the doc under-rates the exposure. "Forage something edible," "build a campfire," "30-second cold plunge," knife work — these can genuinely hurt people, and "with a guide" isn't a shield if the guide is missing.

The fix Tier the risk: most challenges need nothing; a flagged set gets the inline note; a high-risk set (foraging, fire, cold exposure, anything ingestible) gets a mandatory tap-through acknowledgment and should lean toward "learn to identify" over "go eat it." Run the risky drawers past an actual lawyer before launch. Add a blanket, plain-English "use common sense, you're responsible for you" term.
Why "An app told me this mushroom was safe" is a lawsuit and a press disaster. Tiering keeps the calm, note-not-wall UX for the 95% of harmless challenges while putting a real speed-bump on the few that can actually cause injury — proportionate friction, legal cover, and it barely touches the everyday experience.
Privacy & data4/10

Barely addressed, and the app inherently touches sensitive stuff: photos of your home, your food, possibly your kids; location for outdoor/foraging; a behavioral log of everything you do. "Private by default" is stated once and never architected.

The fix Decide the storage model explicitly: on-device-first for the log and photos, with cloud sync as an opt-in (this also answers the "survives a lost phone" question honestly). Minimize location to a coarse, on-demand grab. Publish a short, human-readable privacy promise — "your curios live on your phone unless you ask us to back them up." Make it a selling point.
Why A calm, wholesome, anti-attention-economy app that quietly hoovers up photos and location would be a brand contradiction and a regulatory risk (GDPR/COPPA). Privacy-as-a-feature is both the ethically consistent choice and a marketing asset against the surveillance-y incumbents you're positioning against.
Technical MVP feasibility6/10

The app shell is very buildable — a swipe deck, local storage, a share-card generator (you already have html-to-image and a ShareCard pattern in your other project). The risk isn't the code; it's scope creep: gallery, social, accounts, notifications, weather, and 2,000 strings of content all at once is a year, not a weekend.

The fix Ruthless MVP: local-only, no accounts, no gallery, no social. Ship the loop (taste quiz → daily curio + Right-Now tier → do → stamp → share card → widget) with 50 hand-authored challenges. Prove people come back for a week before building anything that needs a backend. Reuse your existing React/Vite/Tailwind + share-card stack rather than starting cold.
Why The single riskiest assumption is "will people do a real-world challenge daily instead of scrolling?" — and you can test that with a fully local app and zero backend. Everything social/account-based is expensive, slow, and premature until that core question is answered yes.
06

Brainstorm — bigger swings

Ideas beyond patching what's there. Rated by swing size, not by how safe they are.

The "Curio Cabinet" as the whole progression system

Easy · high payoff

Replace streaks-as-anxiety with a literal visual cabinet that fills with tiny illustrated tokens — one per skill tried. Each drawer that fills unlocks a "you've dabbled in all of Fermentation" moment. This is the brand metaphor, it's growth-only (no punishment), and a screenshot of your full cabinet is a far better share than a streak number. It ties naming, progression, sharing, and the anti-shame stance into one object.

"Dabble together" — a shared daily curio for two

Medium · retention

You and a friend/partner get the same challenge each day and see each other's stamp + photo. It's the BeReal social loop but built around doing, not posting. Enormous retention lever (you show up because they're waiting), and it makes the "challenge a friend" viral loop into an ongoing relationship instead of a one-off.

Weather- & time-reactive "smart deck"

Easy · feels magic

The deck quietly reshuffles to the moment: rainy evening → indoor + cozy; bright Saturday morning → outdoor + adventurous; 11pm on the couch → a Right-Now micro-curio. Cheap to build (one weather call + tags), and it's the difference between "a static list" and "an app that knows my life." Punches way above its cost.

Seasonal "Curio Packs" as the content and revenue rhythm

Medium · monetization

Ship themed drops — "Cozy Winter," "Get Outside," "Holiday Gifts You Can Make," "New Year, New Skill." Solves three problems at once: keeps the catalog feeling alive and dated (retention), gives Curio+ a reason to exist (each pack is a premium unlock), and gives you a natural content-production cadence instead of an infinite backlog.

"Skill trees," not just a flat catalog

Big · depth engine

When someone loves a drawer, offer a guided path: cooking → knife skills → one sauce → one braise → host a dinner. Turns Curio from a discovery toy into a learning tool with real depth — directly answering "if someone really likes culinary, give them more." This is the graduation from "dabble" to "get good," and it's what makes the app matter past month one. Big build, so it's a phase-2 bet.

Physical companion: a deck of Curio cards

Medium · brand + revenue

A real printed card deck of challenges — screen-free, giftable, a marketing object that lives on a coffee table. It's on-brand (anti-doomscroll → literally not a screen), a second revenue line, and a Trojan horse that drives app installs via a QR on the box. Low-tech, high-charm.

"Reflection, not just rating" — a one-line note per curio

Easy · emotional depth

Alongside the 1–5 stamp, an optional one-line "what happened." Over months this becomes a diary of a more interesting year — "the year I tried 200 things" — which is a genuinely moving artifact and a killer year-in-review share (Spotify Wrapped, but for your life). Nearly free to build, disproportionately meaningful.

The one thing to internalize

Curio's biggest risk isn't any single feature — it's that the two hardest problems (authoring hundreds of great challenges, and actually intercepting the scroll moment) are the ones the doc spends the least time on, while the fun parts (taxonomy, share cards) are already strong. Spend your next month on a Right-Now tier and 50 lovingly-written challenges, not on the gallery or the social graph. Prove the habit with a local app first; earn the right to build everything else.
