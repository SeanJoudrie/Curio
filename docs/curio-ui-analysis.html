UI DESIGN ANALYSIS · SCREEN-BY-SCREEN · v0.3 MOCKS

Curio — UI Design Analysis

Not product strategy this time — pixels. Annotated mockups of the six screens that carry the app, a read on the core design tension, and the cross-cutting calls: type scale, thumb ergonomics, contrast, motion, and iconography. Every mock is drawn in Curio's own design language so the critique is against something real.
01

The one tension that governs every screen

Before any single screen, the identity has a fork in it. The "specimen-drawer / card-catalog" aesthetic — warm paper, serif + monospace, faded archival palette — is gorgeous and unusually mature. But it was designed as a document look, and this is a young person's daily mobile ritual competing with TikTok's saturation and Duolingo's bounce.

The strength

Archival calm is the anti-doomscroll

The muted, paper-stock, un-neon look is the pitch made visible — it feels like the opposite of a slot machine. Nobody else in the category looks like this, so it's instantly recognizable and it signals "premium, considered, calm." This is a genuine brand asset; don't trade it away.

The risk

Museum can read as "old" on a phone

Serif-heavy, low-saturation, sepia UI can skew mature/literary to a 16–28 audience raised on vivid, springy mobile apps. The danger isn't ugliness — it's feeling quiet to the point of lifeless, and losing the playful joy the copy promises ("rate it a 5 if you laughed").

The resolution running through every mock below: keep the calm paper foundation, but spend the app's energy budget on motion and one or two brighter accent moments (the stamp, a token landing in the cabinet, a confetti-free but satisfying completion) — so it feels alive and tactile without becoming loud. Calm ≠ inert. That's the needle to thread.

Ergonomics: is everything in reach?

A daily one-handed app lives or dies on the thumb zone. Phones are big; the top third is a stretch. Here's the map against the current layouts.

Hard reach
OK
Natural thumb
Primary CTAs (Start, Stamp & log, Share) all sit at the bottom — squarely in the natural thumb zone. Correct across every screen.
Bottom tab bar is reachable and standard. No complaints.
The mood chip and back button live in the hard-reach top corners. Fine for occasional taps, but if budget-switching is frequent, consider a bottom sheet instead of a top-corner control.
The 1–5 stamps sit dead-center — reachable, but tight spacing near the middle risks mis-taps. Widen the gap and size up to 48px.
On the detail screen, key info (lesson/task) is up top where it's read, not tapped — that's fine. Just never put a control up there.

Type system: strong idea, needs a floor

The serif-display / monospace-data pairing is distinctive and right for the brand. The problem isn't the faces — it's that the monospace labels get pushed to 8–10px, under the mobile legibility floor. Here's a proposed scale with a hard minimum.

The rule Nothing below 11px, ever, and monospace (which reads smaller than sans at the same px) gets a 12px floor. Reserve monospace for eyebrows, tags, and stats — never body copy. Set body in the sans at 15px for comfortable reading at arm's length.
05

Color, contrast & motion

Palette & brand distinctiveness8/10

Teal / ochre / rust on warm paper is genuinely differentiated and coherent in both themes. It's the app's biggest UI asset. Keep it.

Last mile Add one brighter "joy" accent reserved for celebration moments (the plum used in this doc, or a warmer gold) so completion can pop against the calm without breaking it. Right now everything is equally muted, which flattens the emotional peaks.
Contrast / accessibility (WCAG)6/10

The low-saturation palette is a contrast risk: --ink-soft on --paper-card is comfortable but the faded ochre tags and mono labels flirt with failing AA (4.5:1) for small text — exactly the elements already too small.

The fix Run every text/background pair through a contrast checker at target size; darken tag text or the muted greys until small text clears 4.5:1 and large text clears 3:1. Muted ≠ low-contrast — you can keep the calm palette and still be legible. Never encode meaning in color alone (the mute/boost states need an icon or label too).
Motion & tactility5/10

This is where the "museum feels dead" risk actually bites. Static, the paper aesthetic can feel inert. Motion is how you prove it's calm, not lifeless — and it's almost entirely unspecified.

The fix Invest in three signature motions: (1) the stamp — a satisfying press-down + rotate + haptic thunk, the app's defining moment; (2) the deal — cards sliding/settling like real cards when you deal a new hand; (3) the token landing — a specimen easing into its cabinet slot on completion. Calm easing, no bounce-fest, always respecting prefers-reduced-motion. These three carry the whole "alive but calm" thesis.
Iconography consistency6/10

The design system correctly specifies etched Lucide line icons, never emoji — but the concept mocks (and the taste-quiz here) lean on emoji as placeholders, which breaks the premium feel instantly.

The fix Commit fully to the line-icon set across tabs, categories, and states — consistent stroke weight, no emoji anywhere in-product. Emoji are fine in user-generated reflections, never in the app's own chrome.
06

Density, hierarchy & the states nobody mocks

Visual hierarchy & focus7/10

Mostly strong — one dominant element per screen, clear labeled sections. The main slip is the Today screen's two competing CTAs (noted on the mock).

The rule One primary action per screen, one hero element, everything else visibly secondary. Audit every screen for "what's the ONE thing," and make sure only one element has the filled-button weight.
Loading / empty / error states4/10

Invisible in every mock, and they're 100% of some sessions: exhausted deck, empty cabinet, offline, image still generating. Undesigned, these default to blank screens or spinners — the least on-brand moments possible.

The fix Design each as a warm, illustrated, in-voice moment: empty cabinet → "your first specimen goes here"; exhausted deck → "that's today — nicely done"; offline → "no signal, but here's a cached one." Skeleton loaders in paper tones, never a generic spinner. These states are where a premium app separates from a template.
Dark mode7/10

The token system already handles both themes cleanly (this very document proves it). Deep forest-paper dark mode is atmospheric and on-brand — a real plus for an evening-use app.

Last mile Re-check the accent-on-dark contrast for the celebration/joy accent and the share-card, and make sure token art reads on both grounds. Don't just invert — tune.
▲

Top 5 UI fixes

If the visual design gets five changes before build, these.

1
Budget for motion & haptics now. The stamp, the deal, the token-landing. This is the single thing standing between "calm and premium" and "pretty but dead." It's not polish — it's the thesis.
2
Commission the token art system. The Cabinet is your signature screen and your best share; flat colored squares turn it into a settings list. Parametric illustrated specimens per skill.
3
Set a type floor (11px / 12px mono) and fix contrast. The charming monospace is currently illegibly small and flirts with WCAG failure. Cheap, high-impact, and an accessibility must.
4
One primary action per screen. Kill the competing-CTA problem on Today; demote secondary actions to text links so the eye always knows the one thing to do.
5
Design the empty/offline/loading states in-voice. They're a third of real sessions and currently blank. This is where premium is won or lost.

The UI verdict

The bones are genuinely strong — this is a rare concept with a real, differentiated visual identity already. The layouts are sound, the hierarchy is mostly right, and the calm paper aesthetic is a true asset in a category of screaming apps. The gap is entirely in the living layer: motion, tactility, token art, and the small-but-critical craft of type floors, contrast, and unglamorous states. Get those right and the "museum feels old" risk flips into "wow, an app that feels calm and alive" — which nobody else in the space has pulled off. The identity is 80% there; the remaining 20% is all motion and craft, not redesign.
